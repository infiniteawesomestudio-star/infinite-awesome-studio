## PROMPT 1: Annual Benefits Program Summary

**Roles:** HR Executive, Benefits Analyst
**The full picture. Once a year. In a format leadership will actually read.**

### What It Solves

Once a year, HR needs to give leadership the complete view of the benefits program: what the company offers, what it costs, how it compares to the market, what employees actually use, and what decisions need to be made for next year. Most HR teams either skip this entirely or produce a spreadsheet nobody reads before the meeting. This prompt generates a narrative summary that covers the whole program — health, dental, vision, life, disability, voluntary, retirement, EAP — in a format a CFO can absorb in five minutes and engage with.

---

### Step 1: Gather

- Full list of benefits offered: medical, dental, vision, life, disability, voluntary, EAP, 401k or retirement, and any additional perks or programs
- Total employer cost for each benefit category (annual)
- Employee participation rates where available
- Any benchmarking data: how your program compares to industry or competitors (even rough data helps)
- What changed this year vs. prior year
- What is working well (high utilization, employee feedback, retention wins)
- What needs attention (low utilization, cost pressure, gaps vs. market)
- Decisions or investments you are recommending for next year
- Any additional documents: benefits guide, carrier summaries, utilization reports, prior year summary, etc.

---

### Step 2: Paste This Into a New Chat

```
You are a senior HR executive preparing an annual benefits program summary for leadership (CEO, CFO, and relevant department heads).

Here is the program data:

ORGANIZATION: [NAME] | [INDUSTRY] | [HEADCOUNT] employees
PLAN YEAR: [DATES]

BENEFITS OFFERED AND EMPLOYER COST:
Medical: [Plan names and types] | Employer cost: $[X]/year total | $[X]/employee/year
Dental: [Carrier] | Employer cost: $[X]/year | Covered: [yes/no for employees/dependents]
Vision: [Carrier] | Employer cost: $[X]/year | Covered: [yes/no]
Life Insurance: [Basic amount] | Employer cost: $[X]/year
Disability: [STD/LTD details] | Employer cost: $[X]/year
EAP: [Provider] | Employer cost: $[X]/year
Retirement: [401k match: X% up to X%] | Employer cost: $[X]/year
Voluntary Benefits: [List if applicable]
Other: [Perks, stipends, wellness programs, etc.]
TOTAL EMPLOYER BENEFITS SPEND: $[X]/year | $[X]/employee/year

PARTICIPATION (where known):
Medical: [X]% enrolled | [X]% waived
Dental: [X]% enrolled
401k: [X]% participating | Average contribution: [X]%
[Other programs as available]

BENCHMARKING (if available):
[e.g., "Industry average total benefits cost is $[X]/employee. We are [above/below/at] market on medical. Below market on [dental/vision/retirement]." or "No formal benchmarking this cycle"]

WHAT CHANGED THIS YEAR:
[e.g., "New medical carrier. Added EAP. 401k match increased from 3% to 4%."]

WHAT IS WORKING:
[e.g., "High 401k participation — 84%. EAP utilization up 40% since adding mental health focus."]

WHAT NEEDS ATTENTION:
[e.g., "Medical cost increased 11% year-over-year. Dental participation is low at 61%. Vision plan has not been updated in 4 years."]

RECOMMENDATIONS FOR NEXT YEAR:
[e.g., "Restructure medical plan design to offset cost trend. Add voluntary dental buy-up. Conduct market review of vision plan."]

Your job:
Write an annual benefits program summary that:
1. Opens with a headline: the state of the benefits program in one sentence
2. Summarizes total investment and per-employee cost
3. Highlights what is working and why it matters (retention, utilization, employee value)
4. Calls out what needs attention — honestly, with the business implication
5. Connects the program to the organization's talent and retention strategy
6. Closes with a clear recommendation section and the decisions needed from leadership

CONSTRAINTS:
- Narrative format throughout — no tables, no spreadsheets
- Write for a CFO and CEO, not an HR audience — no benefits jargon without plain-language explanation
- 500 words maximum
- Be direct about gaps — leadership needs the real picture to make good decisions
- Every cost figure must come from the data provided

FORMAT:

ANNUAL BENEFITS PROGRAM SUMMARY | [ORG NAME] | [PLAN YEAR]

THE STATE OF OUR BENEFITS PROGRAM
[Headline sentence + 2-3 sentences of context]

WHAT WE INVEST
[Total cost + per-employee breakdown + year-over-year change]

WHAT IS WORKING
[2-3 sentences — specific programs, participation rates, outcomes]

WHAT NEEDS ATTENTION
[2-3 sentences — honest assessment of gaps or cost pressure]

HOW WE COMPARE TO THE MARKET
[2-3 sentences — benchmarking context, even if rough]

WHY THIS MATTERS FOR [ORG NAME]
[2-3 sentences connecting benefits to talent strategy, retention, or recruiting]

RECOMMENDATIONS AND DECISIONS NEEDED
[Bulleted list — what you are proposing and what leadership needs to approve]
```

---

### Step 3: You'll Get

A complete, leadership-ready annual summary that covers the full program in plain language. The kind of document that gets the benefits conversation elevated from a line item to a strategic discussion.

## PROMPT 2: Benefits Change Proposal

**Roles:** HR Executive, Broker/Account Manager
**You know what needs to change. This is how you make the case.**

### What It Solves

Adding a benefit. Restructuring cost-sharing. Moving to a new carrier. Introducing a wellness program. Every significant benefits change requires a business case before leadership will approve it — and a spreadsheet full of numbers is not a business case. This prompt generates the proposal: what you are recommending, why it makes sense for the business, what it costs, what the return looks like, and what the risk is if you do nothing. The document that turns a good idea into a decision.

---

### Step 1: Gather

- What you are proposing (be specific — the change, not just the category)
- The problem it solves or the opportunity it addresses
- Cost of the proposed change (annual employer cost, per employee, or net cost if replacing something)
- Expected benefit: retention impact, utilization improvement, cost offset, compliance resolution, or other
- Any data supporting the case (employee feedback, turnover data, benchmarking, utilization trends)
- What happens if you do not make this change (the cost of inaction)
- Implementation timeline and any dependencies
- The specific approval or decision needed from leadership
- Any additional documents: benchmarking reports, vendor proposals, employee survey data, etc.

---

### Step 2: Paste This Into a New Chat

```
You are an HR executive or benefits broker building a business case to propose a benefits change to leadership.

Here is the situation:

ORGANIZATION: [NAME] | [HEADCOUNT] employees
PROPOSING TO: [e.g., "CEO and CFO" or "Executive Leadership Team" or "Board HR Committee"]

THE PROPOSAL:
[Be specific — e.g., "Add an Employee Assistance Program (EAP) with mental health support" or "Move from fully insured to level-funded health plan" or "Introduce a $100/month wellness reimbursement benefit" or "Restructure medical cost-sharing: increase employee contribution by $25/month to offset renewal increase"]

THE PROBLEM THIS SOLVES:
[e.g., "Mental health claims are up 31% year-over-year and our current plan has no dedicated support resource" or "Fully insured renewal is 9% — level-funding projects to save $180K annually based on our claims experience"]

COST OF THE CHANGE:
- Annual employer cost: $[X]
- Per employee per month: $[X]
- Net cost vs. status quo: [e.g., "Net savings of $180K after vendor fees" or "Additional investment of $42K/year"]

EXPECTED RETURN:
[e.g., "Reduce voluntary turnover by 5% — at $8K average replacement cost, saves $160K annually at current headcount" or "Offset 4 points of the medical renewal increase" or "Resolves compliance gap identified in last audit"]

DATA SUPPORTING THIS:
[e.g., "Employee survey: 67% cited mental health support as a top benefits gap" or "Claims analysis: 3 of our top 5 cost drivers are conditions with strong wellness program outcomes" or "Competitor benchmarking: 84% of peer companies offer this benefit"]

COST OF INACTION:
[e.g., "Without this change, we absorb the full 9% medical increase at a cost of $220K annually" or "Turnover in the engineering team costs more than the proposed benefit" or "Compliance exposure if not addressed before next audit"]

TIMELINE:
[e.g., "Can implement by next plan year if approved by [DATE]" or "90-day implementation — vendor selection, communication, enrollment"]

DECISION NEEDED:
[e.g., "Approval to proceed with vendor selection and budget allocation of $[X]" or "Authorization to negotiate level-funding arrangement with current TPA"]

Your job:
Write a benefits change proposal that:
1. Opens with the recommendation — state it in the first sentence
2. Describes the problem or opportunity clearly and briefly
3. Makes the financial case: cost of the change vs. cost of inaction
4. Supports the case with the data provided
5. Acknowledges the key risk or trade-off honestly
6. Closes with a single, specific ask

CONSTRAINTS:
- Lead with the recommendation — do not build to it
- Ground every claim in the data provided — do not assert ROI that is not supported
- Acknowledge the main objection leadership will raise and address it directly
- Under 400 words
- One ask at the end

FORMAT:

BENEFITS CHANGE PROPOSAL | [ORG NAME] | [DATE]

THE RECOMMENDATION
[One sentence]

THE PROBLEM
[2-3 sentences]

THE FINANCIAL CASE
Cost of change: $[X]
Cost of inaction: $[X]
Net position: [Better/worse by $X or other quantified outcome]

WHY THE DATA SUPPORTS THIS
[2-3 bullet points]

THE MAIN OBJECTION — AND THE ANSWER
[Objection leadership will raise + your response]

THE RISK OF WAITING
[1-2 sentences]

WHAT WE NEED
[Single ask + timeline]
```

---

### Step 3: You'll Get

A proposal that leads with what you want, grounds it in numbers, and anticipates the pushback. The kind of document that gets a decision in the meeting instead of a "let us think about it."

---

### Time Saved

| Step | Without Prompt | With Prompt |
|---|---|---|
| Building the business case narrative | 45 min | — |
| Anticipating and addressing objections | 20 min | — |
| Gathering inputs + editing output | — | 15 min |
| **Total** | **~65 min** | **~15 min** |

**50 minutes saved per proposal. And a better shot at getting the yes.**

## PROMPT 3: Broker Annual Client Review Summary

**Roles:** Broker/Account Manager
**What did we actually do for this client this year? Say it clearly.**

### What It Solves

At the end of every plan year, a broker should be able to answer one question: what did we do for this client, and was it worth their trust? Most brokers do not have a formal document for this. They rely on the relationship, the renewal call, and the hope that the client remembers the fire you put out in February. This prompt generates the annual client review summary — what you delivered, what you saved or improved, what is coming next year, and why the relationship is worth continuing. The document that earns the renewal before the renewal conversation even starts.

---

### Step 1: Gather

- Client name and plan year
- Key services delivered this year (stewardship report, renewal negotiation, OE support, compliance tracking, employee communications, vendor management, etc.)
- Measurable outcomes where available (renewal rate achieved vs. proposed, dollars saved, issues resolved)
- Any problems you solved mid-year (claims issue, carrier dispute, compliance question, OE problem)
- What changed or improved in their benefits program this year
- What is on the horizon for next year (market conditions, compliance changes, plan review)
- Any investments you made in the relationship beyond standard service (additional resources, research, access to tools)
- Any additional documents: stewardship report, renewal summary, service log, communication history, etc.

---

### Step 2: Paste This Into a New Chat

```
You are a benefits broker writing an annual client review summary to present to your client at the end of the plan year.

Here is the information:

CLIENT: [NAME] | [INDUSTRY] | [GROUP SIZE] employees
PLAN YEAR: [DATES]
PRIMARY CONTACT: [NAME, TITLE]

SERVICES DELIVERED THIS YEAR:
[List what you did — e.g.:]
- Stewardship report delivered [DATE]
- Renewal negotiated: carrier proposed [X]%, achieved [X]%
- Open enrollment support: [X] employee sessions, communications drafted and distributed
- Compliance calendar maintained: [list deadlines tracked]
- Mid-year QLE processing support
- [Other services]

MEASURABLE OUTCOMES:
[e.g., "Negotiated renewal from 9.2% to 4.1% — saved $[X]/year" or "Resolved carrier billing dispute resulting in $[X] credit" or "100% SBC distribution completed before OE deadline" or "Reduced employee benefits questions to HR by approximately 30% through improved OE communication"]

PROBLEMS SOLVED MID-YEAR:
[e.g., "Large claimant case management referral in March — connected with carrier case manager, monitored through Q2" or "Carrier network issue for specialty provider — resolved in 18 days" or "None significant this year"]

WHAT IMPROVED THIS YEAR:
[e.g., "Added EAP. Improved OE communication — fewer employee questions than prior year. Stewardship report delivered 2 weeks earlier than last year."]

WHAT IS COMING NEXT YEAR:
[e.g., "Market conditions: medical trend projected at 7-9% nationally. ACA reporting changes effective [DATE]. Recommend early renewal strategy discussion by [DATE]." ]

INVESTMENT BEYOND STANDARD SERVICE:
[e.g., "Provided access to AI workflow tools for HR team" or "Conducted two lunch-and-learn sessions for employees" or "None beyond standard service this year"]

Your job:
Write an annual client review summary that:
1. Opens with the year in one sentence — what defined this plan year for this client
2. Summarizes what was delivered and what it produced
3. Calls out the one or two moments where you went beyond the standard and why it mattered
4. Looks ahead: what is on the horizon and why they should act now
5. Closes with a statement of the relationship — not salesy, just direct about the value

CONSTRAINTS:
- This is not a sales pitch. It is a professional accounting of what was delivered
- Let the outcomes speak — do not oversell or use superlatives
- Be honest if a year was quiet — a quiet year without problems is a good year
- Under 400 words
- Warm but professional tone — this is a relationship document

FORMAT:

[CLIENT NAME] | ANNUAL BENEFITS REVIEW | [PLAN YEAR]

THIS YEAR IN ONE SENTENCE
[The defining theme or outcome of the plan year]

WHAT WE DELIVERED
[Bullet list: service + outcome for each]

WHERE IT MADE A DIFFERENCE
[2-3 sentences on 1-2 standout moments — specific, not vague]

WHAT IS COMING
[2-3 sentences on next year's landscape and what to watch]

THE ROAD AHEAD
[1-2 sentences — your commitment to what comes next, stated plainly]
```

---

### Step 3: You'll Get

A client review document that reminds them — before the renewal conversation starts — exactly what you have done and why the relationship is worth continuing. Not a pitch. A record.
