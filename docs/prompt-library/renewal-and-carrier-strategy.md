## PROMPT 1: Carrier Renewal Negotiation Brief

**Roles:** Broker/Account Manager, Benefits Analyst
**Walk in with a position. Not a question.**

### What It Solves

Walking into a carrier renewal negotiation without a written brief is walking in to react instead of drive. You know the data. You know what the client needs. But without a structured prep document — what you are asking for, what the data supports, what you will accept, and where you walk away — the carrier controls the conversation. This prompt generates that brief before the call.

---

### Step 1: Gather

- Current carrier name, plan type, and years with the carrier
- Proposed renewal rate increase (% and total dollar impact)
- Claims summary: loss ratio, PMPM trend, year-over-year change
- What you are asking for: specific rate reduction target, plan design concessions, added services or credits
- Your leverage: group size, claims experience, years of tenure, market alternatives you are actively pursuing
- Competing quotes or market intel, if available
- Client's top priority: cost containment, plan stability, network breadth, or other
- Walk-away threshold: what would make you move the group to a new carrier
- Any additional documents: current contract, renewal proposal, stewardship report, competing quotes, etc.

---

### Step 2: Paste This Into a New Chat

```
You are a senior benefits broker preparing for a carrier renewal negotiation on behalf of a client.

Here is the situation:

CLIENT: [NAME], [INDUSTRY], [GROUP SIZE] employees
CARRIER: [NAME]
YEARS WITH CARRIER: [X]
PLAN TYPE: [e.g., "Fully insured PPO" or "Level-funded HDHP"]

RENEWAL PROPOSAL:
- Proposed increase: [X]%
- Dollar impact: +$[X]/month total | +$[X]/employee/month
- Carrier's stated reason: [e.g., "Claims trend, pharmacy increase, market adjustment"]

CLAIMS SUMMARY:
- Loss ratio: [X]% (claims paid vs. premium collected)
- PMPM trend: [Prior year $X] vs. [Current year $X] — change: +/-[X]%
- Notable drivers: [e.g., "Two large claimants drove 34% of spend" or "Pharmacy up 22%"]

WHAT WE ARE ASKING FOR:
- Rate target: [e.g., "Counter at 4% or less" or "Flat renewal"]
- Plan design ask: [e.g., "Maintain current deductible" or "Add HSA compatibility"]
- Other asks: [e.g., "12-month rate guarantee" or "Wellness credits" or "None"]

OUR LEVERAGE:
- [e.g., "Group has been with carrier 7 years with consistently favorable loss ratio"]
- [e.g., "We have a competitive quote from [CARRIER] at [X]% lower"]
- [e.g., "Group size makes this a meaningful account — [X] covered lives"]

CLIENT PRIORITY: [e.g., "Cost containment first. Network stability second. Will consider plan design changes if it moves the number."]

WALK-AWAY POINT: [e.g., "Anything over 6% and we go to market" or "If they will not move on the deductible, we move the group"]

COMPETING INTEL: [e.g., "Quote from Aetna at 3.2% increase, similar network" or "Have not gone to market yet but will if needed"]

Your job:
Build a renewal negotiation brief that:
1. Summarizes our position in 2-3 sentences — what we are asking for and why
2. Lays out the data that supports our counter (loss ratio, claims trend, tenure)
3. Lists our asks in priority order: must-haves, nice-to-haves, walk-aways
4. Identifies the carrier's likely objections and how to respond to each
5. States the walk-away threshold clearly
6. Suggests how to open the call and how to close it

CONSTRAINTS:
- Ground every ask in data — do not make requests that the claims experience does not support
- Be direct about leverage without being combative — the goal is a deal, not a fight
- If the claims data does not support a strong counter, say so and suggest a realistic ask instead
- Do NOT invent claims numbers or market rates
- Format this as a working document I can reference during the call

FORMAT:

OUR POSITION
[2-3 sentence summary]

DATA SUPPORTING OUR COUNTER
[Bullet list of data points]

ASKS — PRIORITY ORDER
Must-have: [X]
Nice-to-have: [X]
Walk-away: [X]

CARRIER'S LIKELY OBJECTIONS
Objection: [X] | Our response: [X]
Objection: [X] | Our response: [X]

WALK-AWAY THRESHOLD
[Clear statement]

HOW TO OPEN THE CALL
[1-2 sentences]

HOW TO CLOSE THE CALL
[1-2 sentences — what agreement or next step you are driving toward]
```

---

### Step 3: You'll Get

A negotiation brief you can open on a second screen during the call. Your position grounded in data, your asks prioritized, their objections anticipated, and your walk-away clearly defined before you pick up the phone.

## PROMPT 2: Competing Quotes Comparison

**Roles:** Broker/Account Manager, Benefits Analyst
**The cheapest quote is not always the right quote.**

### What It Solves

When you have gone to market and have two or three quotes, comparing them is harder than it looks. Premium is the easy part. The harder question is network adequacy for where your employees actually live, plan design differences that shift costs from premium to out-of-pocket, formulary gaps that affect employees on specialty drugs, and the disruption risk of moving an established group. This prompt takes raw quote data and builds a structured comparison that surfaces the real differences — not just the lowest number.

---

### Step 1: Gather

- Current carrier and plan as the baseline (for comparison)
- Quote 1: carrier name, plan type, premium by tier, network name, key plan design details
- Quote 2: same format
- Quote 3 if applicable: same format
- Where employees are located (zip codes, metro areas, or states — affects network adequacy)
- Any known utilization patterns worth flagging (specialists, facilities, ongoing conditions)
- Client's stated priority: cost, network breadth, plan stability, HSA compatibility, or other
- Any plan design differences that affect total cost beyond premium (deductible, OOP max, Rx tiers)
- Any additional documents: carrier proposals, SBCs, network directories, formularies, etc.

---

### Step 2: Paste This Into a New Chat

```
You are a benefits broker comparing carrier quotes for a client considering their renewal options.

Here is the data:

CLIENT: [NAME], [INDUSTRY], [GROUP SIZE] employees
EMPLOYEE LOCATIONS: [e.g., "Primarily MD, VA, DC metro" or "Multi-state: MD, TX, CA, NY"]
CLIENT PRIORITY: [e.g., "Keep costs down without disrupting network access" or "HSA-compatible plan required"]

CURRENT CARRIER (BASELINE):
- Carrier: [NAME]
- Plan: [TYPE]
- Monthly premium by tier: EE $[X] | EE+Sp $[X] | EE+Ch $[X] | Family $[X]
- Deductible: $[X] individual / $[X] family
- OOP Max: $[X] individual / $[X] family
- Network: [NAME]
- Rx: [Tier structure or formulary notes]
- Renewal increase: [X]%

QUOTE 1:
- Carrier: [NAME]
- Plan: [TYPE]
- Monthly premium by tier: EE $[X] | EE+Sp $[X] | EE+Ch $[X] | Family $[X]
- Deductible: $[X] individual / $[X] family
- OOP Max: $[X] individual / $[X] family
- Network: [NAME]
- Rx: [Tier structure or formulary notes]
- Notable differences from current: [e.g., "Higher deductible, lower premium" or "Narrower network"]

QUOTE 2:
[Same format]

QUOTE 3 (if applicable):
[Same format]

UTILIZATION FLAGS (if known):
[e.g., "Several employees use oncology specialists at [HOSPITAL SYSTEM] — need to confirm in-network" or "High specialty pharmacy spend — formulary comparison critical" or "No known flags"]

Your job:
Build a side-by-side quote comparison that:
1. Shows premium cost side-by-side across all options, by tier
2. Compares total cost of coverage (premium + likely out-of-pocket based on plan design)
3. Flags network adequacy concerns based on employee locations
4. Identifies plan design differences that shift cost from premium to employee out-of-pocket
5. Notes any formulary or Rx differences worth flagging
6. Scores each option against the client's stated priority
7. Surfaces the top 2-3 questions to resolve before making a recommendation

CONSTRAINTS:
- Do NOT recommend a carrier based on incomplete data — flag gaps instead
- Be explicit when a lower premium comes with a trade-off (higher OOP, narrower network, etc.)
- Do NOT assume network adequacy without location data — flag it if not confirmed
- Present the data neutrally — the recommendation comes after the client reviews this
- If data is missing for any quote, write [DATA NEEDED] and note what is required

FORMAT:

PREMIUM COMPARISON
[Table or side-by-side: all options by tier]

TOTAL COST OF COVERAGE (ESTIMATED)
[Premium + typical OOP estimate — note assumptions]

NETWORK ADEQUACY
[Current]: [Notes]
[Quote 1]: [Notes]
[Quote 2]: [Notes]

PLAN DESIGN DIFFERENCES
[Key differences that shift cost or coverage]

RX / FORMULARY FLAGS
[Any differences worth flagging]

HOW EACH OPTION SCORES AGAINST CLIENT PRIORITY
[Current]: [Score/notes]
[Quote 1]: [Score/notes]
[Quote 2]: [Score/notes]

QUESTIONS TO RESOLVE BEFORE RECOMMENDING
1. [Question]
2. [Question]
3. [Question]
```

---

### Step 3: You'll Get

A structured comparison that shows the real trade-offs between options — not just the cheapest number. The questions-to-resolve section alone prevents the client conversation from getting derailed by missing information.

---

| Step | Without Prompt | With Prompt |
|---|---|---|
| Building a manual comparison spreadsheet | 45 min | — |
| Writing narrative around the numbers | 20 min | — |
| Gathering inputs + editing output | — | 20 min |
| **Total** | **~65 min** | **~20 min** |

**45 minutes saved per market analysis. And fewer surprises in the client meeting.**

## PROMPT 3: Renewal Recommendation Memo

**Roles:** Broker/Account Manager, HR Executive
**All the analysis is done. Now say what you actually think.**

### What It Solves

After the stewardship work, the carrier negotiation, and the market comparison — someone has to make a recommendation. Not a summary of everything you looked at. A clear position: here is what we recommend, here is why the data supports it, here is what we considered and rejected, here is what you need to decide, and here is the timeline. The memo that turns analysis into action.

---

### Step 1: Gather

- Options evaluated: renewal as-is, plan design change, new carrier, alternative funding strategy, or other
- The recommended option
- Core rationale for the recommendation (in plain terms)
- Dollar impact vs. status quo (what it costs to do nothing vs. the recommendation)
- Key risks of the recommended option and how you would mitigate them
- What was considered and rejected, with a one-line reason for each
- Decision needed from the client or leadership and by when
- Next steps if they approve the recommendation
- Any additional documents: stewardship report, quote comparison, negotiation summary, etc.

---

### Step 2: Paste This Into a New Chat

```
You are a benefits broker or HR executive writing a renewal recommendation memo to a client or leadership team.

Here is the situation:

CLIENT OR ORG: [NAME]
PLAN EFFECTIVE DATE: [DATE]
DECISION DEADLINE: [DATE — when they need to decide to hit the effective date]

OPTIONS EVALUATED:
1. [e.g., "Renew as-is with current carrier at proposed 9.2% increase"]
2. [e.g., "Renew with plan design change: raise deductible from $500 to $1,000, reducing increase to 4.1%"]
3. [e.g., "Move to Aetna — competitive quote at 2.8% above current premium, similar network"]
4. [e.g., "Explore level-funding with current TPA"]

RECOMMENDATION: [e.g., "Option 2 — renew with plan design change"]

WHY WE RECOMMEND THIS:
[3-5 bullet points — the data and reasoning behind the recommendation]
- [e.g., "Claims experience supports the deductible increase — utilization is low, most employees do not hit current deductible"]
- [e.g., "Keeps the group with a known carrier and eliminates disruption risk"]
- [e.g., "Reduces the rate increase by more than half"]

DOLLAR IMPACT vs. STATUS QUO:
- Renew as-is: +$[X]/month total | +$[X]/employee/month
- Recommended option: +$[X]/month total | +$[X]/employee/month
- Difference: $[X]/month saved

KEY RISKS OF RECOMMENDATION:
- [e.g., "Higher deductible increases employee out-of-pocket for those who use healthcare regularly"]
- [e.g., "Mitigation: consider adding a supplemental FSA contribution to offset impact for lower-wage employees"]

WHAT WAS REJECTED AND WHY:
- [Option]: [One-line reason — e.g., "Renew as-is: 9.2% is above what the claims experience supports"]
- [Option]: [One-line reason — e.g., "Aetna quote: network gaps in [LOCATION] create unacceptable disruption risk for current utilizers"]

DECISION NEEDED: [e.g., "Approve plan design change and authorize us to counter the carrier at 4% or below"]
NEXT STEPS IF APPROVED: [e.g., "Counter carrier by [DATE]. Finalize plan documents by [DATE]. OE communications begin [DATE]."]

Your job:
Write a renewal recommendation memo that:
1. Opens with the recommendation — stated clearly in the first sentence
2. Summarizes why the data supports it in plain language
3. Shows the dollar impact in terms leadership can act on
4. Acknowledges the key risk honestly and states how it is managed
5. Briefly explains what was considered and why it was not selected
6. Closes with a single clear ask: what decision is needed and by when

CONSTRAINTS:
- Lead with the recommendation — do not make them read to the end to find out what you think
- Ground every point in data provided — do not editorialize beyond what the numbers support
- Acknowledge trade-offs honestly. A recommendation that hides risks loses trust when those risks show up
- Under 400 words
- One ask at the end — not a list of questions

FORMAT:

RENEWAL RECOMMENDATION | [CLIENT/ORG NAME] | [PLAN YEAR]

RECOMMENDATION
[One clear sentence]

WHY WE RECOMMEND THIS
[3-4 bullet points grounded in data]

DOLLAR IMPACT
[Side-by-side: status quo vs. recommendation]

THE TRADE-OFF
[Honest 2-3 sentences on the main risk + mitigation]

WHAT WE CONSIDERED AND REJECTED
[Bullet list — option + one-line reason]

DECISION NEEDED
[Single ask + deadline]

NEXT STEPS
[3-4 action items with owners and dates]
```

---

### Step 3: You'll Get

A memo that leads with what you actually think, grounds it in data, and asks for one specific decision. The kind of document that gets read before the meeting instead of during it.
